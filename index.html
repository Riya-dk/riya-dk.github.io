Electronics retail: inventory & sales – details

This project uses a star-schema dataset focused on one industry (electronics): products, stores, dates, sales and inventory. All products are electronics (Accessories, Computers, Displays, Phones).

Data source: synthetic electronics retail data that I designed for this project. It is not from a real company and contains no real customer information.

Findings

Across the sample period, the electronics retailer generated around $135k in revenue from nearly 1,000 units sold. Revenue is concentrated in a few key product lines: monitors, business laptops and mid‑range smartphones lead, with accessories contributing a large share through high volume (mice, headphones, power banks). This concentration means that a small number of SKUs drive most of the business; losing availability on any of these top lines has an outsized impact on total sales and customer satisfaction. I therefore focused the analysis on identifying which products and subcategories deserve the most attention for replenishment and safety stock.

By subcategory, Computers and Accessories each drive about 30% of revenue, followed by Displays and Phones. Accessories sell in higher volume at lower unit price, so they are critical for footfall and basket size; Computers and Displays deliver higher revenue per unit and often higher margin, so they are critical to protect from stockouts. Understanding this split helps explain why we need different inventory policies by category: fast reorder cycles for high‑volume accessories, and safety stock or priority allocation for high‑value computers and displays.

Inventory snapshots show stockouts on wireless mice (multiple occurrences), plus webcams and ultrawide monitors. These gaps indicate lost sales risk on popular accessories and high‑margin display lines. Each day a best‑seller is at zero is lost revenue and can push customers to competitors. At the same time, some computers (e.g. gaming laptops) and keyboards have low turnover, suggesting potential overstock or slower demand—candidates for promotions or reduced ordering to free working capital. Holding too much slow‑moving stock increases carrying cost and ties up cash that could be used for faster‑moving or higher‑margin lines.

The analysis supports investment decisions: prioritise replenishment for fast‑moving accessories and protect stock for high‑revenue computers and displays, while reviewing slow‑turnover items for assortment or promotional action. The charts below visualise these findings so that replenishment, merchandising and finance can align on the same priorities.

Charts – what each one shows and why it matters

Recommendations and why

  • Prioritise replenishment for fast‑moving accessories and high‑revenue lines. Stockouts on wireless mice, webcams and ultrawide monitors directly cause lost sales. I recommend setting minimum stock levels or reorder triggers for these SKUs.
  • Protect stock for computers and displays. These subcategories deliver high revenue per unit. I recommend treating them as critical for inventory planning and allocating more safety stock to top products.
  • Review slow‑turnover items for promotions or reduced ordering. Products with low turnover hold working capital. I recommend reviewing them for short‑term promotions or smaller batch sizes so capital can shift toward faster‑moving lines.
  • Use store and region performance to align inventory and staffing. Auckland and Wellington drive the most revenue. I recommend aligning replenishment and staffing to these patterns.

Business questions, SQL & answers

1. Which electronics products drive most revenue?

SELECT
  p.ProductName,
  p.Subcategory,
  SUM(s.Revenue) AS TotalRevenue,
  SUM(s.UnitsSold) AS TotalUnits
FROM FactSales s
JOIN DimProduct p ON s.ProductID = p.ProductID
GROUP BY p.ProductID, p.ProductName, p.Subcategory
ORDER BY TotalRevenue DESC
LIMIT 10;

Result: monitors, business laptops, mid‑range smartphones, desktops and Bluetooth headphones are the top revenue drivers.

2. Where are we experiencing stockouts?

SELECT st.StoreName, st.Region, p.ProductName, p.Subcategory, i.OnHandQty, i.DateKey
FROM FactInventory i
JOIN DimStore st ON i.StoreID = st.StoreID
JOIN DimProduct p ON i.ProductID = p.ProductID
WHERE i.OnHandQty = 0
ORDER BY st.StoreName, p.ProductName;

Result: repeated stockouts on wireless mice, plus webcams and ultrawide monitors. Prioritise replenishment for these lines.

3. Which products are potential overstock (low turnover)?

WITH sales_by_product AS (
  SELECT ProductID, SUM(UnitsSold) AS TotalUnitsSold FROM FactSales GROUP BY ProductID
),
inv_by_product AS (
  SELECT ProductID, AVG(OnHandQty) AS AvgOnHand FROM FactInventory GROUP BY ProductID
)
SELECT p.ProductName, p.Subcategory,
  COALESCE(s.TotalUnitsSold, 0) AS TotalUnitsSold,
  ROUND(COALESCE(inv.AvgOnHand, 0), 2) AS AvgOnHand,
  ROUND(CASE WHEN COALESCE(inv.AvgOnHand, 0) > 0
    THEN 1.0 * s.TotalUnitsSold / inv.AvgOnHand ELSE NULL END, 2) AS InventoryTurnover
FROM DimProduct p
LEFT JOIN sales_by_product s ON p.ProductID = s.ProductID
LEFT JOIN inv_by_product inv ON p.ProductID = inv.ProductID
ORDER BY InventoryTurnover ASC;

Result: gaming laptops, laptop stands, mechanical keyboards and some tablets have lower turnover. Review for promotions or reduced ordering.

4. How does performance vary by store and region?

SELECT st.Region, st.StoreName, SUM(s.Revenue) AS TotalRevenue, SUM(s.UnitsSold) AS TotalUnits
FROM FactSales s
JOIN DimStore st ON s.StoreID = st.StoreID
GROUP BY st.StoreID, st.StoreName, st.Region
ORDER BY st.Region, TotalRevenue DESC;

Result: Auckland and Wellington electronics stores generate the highest revenue. Use to align inventory and staffing across regions.

5. Revenue and margin by subcategory

SELECT p.Subcategory, COUNT(DISTINCT p.ProductID) AS ProductCount,
  SUM(s.Revenue) AS TotalRevenue, SUM(s.UnitsSold) AS TotalUnits,
  ROUND(SUM(s.Revenue) - SUM(s.UnitsSold * p.Cost), 2) AS GrossProfit
FROM FactSales s
JOIN DimProduct p ON s.ProductID = p.ProductID
GROUP BY p.Subcategory
ORDER BY TotalRevenue DESC;

Result: Computers and Accessories lead by revenue; Displays and Phones follow. Gross profit by subcategory helps decide where to invest in stock.

SaaS revenue, churn & unit economics – details

Financial analytics for a SaaS business: MRR, churn, ARPU, and gross margin by plan and segment. The Excel workbook includes raw data sheets, pivot-style summaries (MRR by plan/segment, costs by category), and bar/pie charts for insights.

Data source: synthetic subscription and cost data designed for this project. Not from a real company.

Download Excel dashboard (with pivot-style summaries & charts)

Findings

Current MRR is ~$1,692 across 20 active customers, with ARPU around $84.60. Enterprise and Professional plans drive the majority of revenue, while Starter plans contribute volume. This mix matters because it tells us where revenue is concentrated: a small number of higher‑value accounts (Enterprise and Professional) generate most of the recurring income, so losing even one of them has a material impact on the top line. At the same time, Starter customers represent the largest headcount and often the entry point for future upsell; keeping them engaged and moving them to Professional is a key lever for growth.

Churn events highlight risk in the SMB segment; retention efforts and upsell to Professional could improve unit economics. When SMB customers churn, we lose both the immediate MRR and the opportunity to grow them over time. Costs are split across Marketing, Support and Hosting—understanding cost per customer by segment helps prioritise where to invest in growth (e.g. marketing that acquires high‑LTV customers) vs where to reduce spend (e.g. support deflection, hosting efficiency). Without this breakdown, it’s hard to know whether rising costs are justified by revenue or eating into margin.

Charts – what each one shows and why it matters

Recommendations and why

  • Focus retention on high‑value Enterprise accounts. Enterprise plans contribute a large share of MRR, so losing even one Enterprise customer has a bigger impact on revenue than losing several Starter customers. I recommend dedicated account management, regular health checks and proactive outreach for Enterprise accounts, plus early warning (e.g. usage drop, support tickets, contract renewal date) so we can intervene before churn. The reasoning is simple: the chart shows that a small number of high‑value contracts drive the business; protecting them is the highest‑leverage retention activity.
  • Invest in Professional upsell for Starter customers. Moving Starter customers to Professional increases ARPU and typically improves retention because of higher commitment. The MRR‑by‑plan chart shows Professional as a major revenue driver, so growing that tier is a direct lever for revenue. I recommend targeted upsell campaigns, success milestones that trigger upgrade prompts, and packaging that makes the step to Professional clearly valuable. The reasoning: we already have Starter customers; converting a portion of them is often more efficient than acquiring the same MRR from net‑new leads.
  • Monitor cost growth vs revenue to protect gross margin. If Marketing, Support or Hosting costs grow faster than MRR, gross margin falls. I recommend tracking cost per customer and cost‑to‑revenue ratio by segment over time. When a cost category grows, we can then decide whether it’s justified (e.g. marketing that acquires high‑LTV customers) or needs optimisation (e.g. support deflection, hosting efficiency) so the business can scale profitably. The chart gives leadership a single view to have that conversation.

Funnel & campaign analytics – details

Sales funnel analysis: conversion by stage (Lead → Qualified → Proposal → Won) and performance by source.

Data source: synthetic funnel/campaign data designed for this project. Not from a real company.

Findings

The funnel shows 20 leads, with about half converting to 10 won deals. The largest drop-off occurs between Lead and Qualified—many leads never reach the next stage, which means we are either attracting low‑intent prospects or losing them before sales can properly qualify. How risky is this to the business and what can we do to improve without invloving little to no scope creep? Improving qualification criteria, lead scoring and follow‑up speed could help focus effort on higher‑intent prospects and reduce wasted capacity on leads that never convert. Understanding where the drop-off happens is the first step to fixing it.

LinkedIn and Referral drive the most won deals; Website (Organic and Paid Search) and Job Board contribute leads but with varying conversion. This tells us that not all channels are equal: some bring volume, others bring quality. Investing in Referral programs and LinkedIn campaigns appears to have the strongest ROI for this sample because they produce closed business, not just pipeline. Channels that generate many leads but few wins may need better targeting, messaging or handoff to sales so that we either improve conversion or reallocate budget to sources that already convert well.

Charts – What each one shows and why it matters

Recommendations and why

  • Improve Lead → Qualified conversion through better targeting and nurturing. The largest drop-off is between Lead and Qualified, so many leads never reach the next stage. I recommend this because fixing the top of the funnel multiplies through the rest: more qualified leads mean more proposals and more wins without necessarily spending more on acquisition. Actions include clearer qualification criteria, lead scoring so sales focus on high‑intent leads, and automated nurturing so leads don’t go cold before contact. The reasoning is that the funnel chart shows this step as the main bottleneck; improving it has the highest impact on closed revenue.
  • Prioritise channels with the best lead-to-won conversion. LinkedIn and Referral drive the most won deals in this data. I recommend shifting budget and effort toward these channels because they deliver outcomes (revenue), not just volume. At the same time, review channels that bring many leads but few wins—either improve targeting and handoff to sales or reduce spend and reallocate to higher‑converting sources so pipeline quality and efficiency improve. The reasoning: the won‑by‑source chart shows where closed business actually comes from; aligning spend with that is the most direct way to improve ROI.
  • Track time-in-stage to identify bottlenecks. How long prospects sit in each stage affects conversion and forecast accuracy. Long times in Qualified or Proposal can signal process delays, slow follow‑up or misalignment between marketing and sales. I recommend tracking time-in-stage so we can fix slow handoffs, add resources where deals stall, and improve pipeline predictability for the business. The reasoning: without time data, we only see “how many” at each stage, not “how long” they stay—and duration is often where process issues show up.
  • Next steps: automation and scale. Using AI and sales support tools to automate lead follow-up, scoring and reporting can free the team to focus on high-value conversations. Monitoring the funnel and doubling down on the platforms that generate the most (and best) leads will support growth and scale as you run future campaigns.
